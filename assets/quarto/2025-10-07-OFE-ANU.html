Research focus

This study explores statistical strategies for the design and analysis of on-farm experiments (OFE), building on established foundations (Piepho et al. 2011; Pringle et al. 2004) while advancing methodological innovation.

Key objectives

Design layout evaluation through simulation studies (Cao et al. 2024)

Statistical method comparison: GWR and LMM (Rakshit et al. 2020; Stefanova et al. 2023)

Trial type assessment: Large-strip and stacked replicated trials

Data granularity impact on inference accuracy

Integration with global best practices

Experimental design principles of OFE

Illustration of OFE trial designs

Illustration of some OFE trial designs, including strip-based, grid-based, Latin square, and gradient layouts. These designs support different analytical goals, such as treatment comparison, spatial modelling, and rate-response analysis (Li et al. (2023))

Historical context: the Flawborough chequerboard experiment

a) Experimental design:

Design characteristics:

  • Small plots: Individual treatment plots

  • Systematic arrangement: Chequerboard pattern

  • Multiple rates: 0, 120, 240, 360 kg N/ha

  • High replication: Many small experimental units

  • Controlled conditions: Research-station environment

b) Aerial field view:

Practical limitations:

  • Small scale: Not representative of commercial farming

  • Equipment constraints: Requires specialised machinery

  • Edge effects: High border-to-area ratio

  • Implementation cost: Expensive to establish and maintain

  • Farmer adoption: Difficult to scale to commercial operations

Why our focus is on strip trials?

Chequerboard limitations for modern agriculture

  • Scale mismatch: Small plots don’t reflect commercial reality
  • Edge effects: High plot perimeter creates boundary influences
  • Cost-effectiveness: High setup and maintenance costs
  • Farmer engagement: Difficult for farmers to implement

Strip trial advantages for OFE

  • Commercial compatibility: Matches farming operation scale
  • Machinery integration: Works with standard farm equipment
  • Cost efficiency: Lower setup and management costs
  • Farmer adoption: Easy to implement and understand
  • Practical relevance: Direct application to farm operations
  • Precision agriculture: Compatible with variable-rate technology
  • Technology integration: Works with GPS and yield monitors

Methodological evolution

Key transition:

  • 1990s-2000s: Chequerboard and small-plot research dominated

  • 2000s-2010s: Recognition of scale and adoption limitations

  • 2010s-Present: Shift to farm-scale trials, ANOVA, t-test or paired-t test

  • Future direction: Integration with precision agriculture and AI, real time disease detection, yield forecasting etc

Methodological evolution

Our research contribution:

  • Bridge the gap: Maintaining statistical rigor while achieving practical relevance

  • Methodology validation: Delivering robust statistical models and inference

  • Adoption facilitation: Creating methods farmers can actually implement

  • Innovation acceleration: Reducing time from research to practice

A typical strip trial design

Strip dimensions:

  • Width: 10-30m (machinery width dependent)

  • Length: 200m-1km (field size dependent)

  • Area: 0.2-1.5 ha per strip

  • Number: 2-6 treatments typical

Design selection guidelines

According to (Piepho et al. 2011; Pringle et al. 2004):

Use randomised designs when:

Field appears homogeneous

No known spatial trends

Statistical validity is priority

Small to moderate field sizes

Research-focused objectives

Use systematic designs when:

Strong machinery constraints exist

Large commercial fields (>50 ha)

Operational efficiency critical

Known spatial gradients

Farmer adoption is priority

Quick decision guide

For categorical varieties comparison → use randomised designs

For local regression and drawing a prescription map → use systematic designs

Spatial statistics for OFE analysis

Theoretical framework (Cressie 2013)

Fundamental Spatial Concepts

  • First law of geography: “Everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things” (Tobler, 1970)
  • Spatial autocorrelation: Correlation between observations as a function of spatial separation
  • Stationarity assumptions: Statistical properties invariant under spatial translation
  • Isotropy vs anisotropy: Directional independence of spatial correlation

OFE-specific spatial challenges

  • Non-stationary processes: Treatment effects varying across space
  • Anisotropic correlation: Directional differences in spatial dependence
  • Boundary effects: Edge influences on spatial estimation
  • Irregular sampling: Equipment-dependent observation locations

Statistical analysis for OFE - GWR

“Treatment effects are not uniform across fields - spatial analysis reveals where treatments work best” (Rakshit et al. (2020))

Statistical analysis for OFE - GWR

The underlying template model for GWR is given by

𝑦(𝑠𝑖)=𝛽0+∑𝑗=1𝑘𝛽𝑗𝑥𝑗(𝑠𝑖)+𝜀𝑖,y(si)=β0+∑j=1kβjxj(si)+εi,
where 𝛽=(𝛽0,𝛽1,…,𝛽𝑘)⊤β=(β0,β1,…,βk)⊤ are model parameters corresponding to the 𝑘k treatment levels and 𝜀𝑖∼𝑁(0,𝜏2)εi∼N(0,τ2), 𝑖=1,…,𝑛i=1,…,n, are independent and identically distributed error terms at 𝑛n grid points.

For a query location 𝑠s, the local log-likelihood is given by

log𝐿(𝑠;𝛽)=−12𝜏2∑𝑖=1𝑛𝐾(𝑠,𝑠𝑖)(𝑦(𝑠𝑖)−𝛽0−∑𝑗=1𝑘𝛽𝑗𝑥𝑗(𝑠𝑖))2log⁡L(s;β)=−12τ2∑i=1nK(s,si)(y(si)−β0−∑j=1kβjxj(si))2
where 𝐾(⋅,⋅)K(⋅,⋅) is a given kernel function, such as Gaussian, exponential, bi-square or tri-cube Gollini et al. (2015).

Statistical analysis for OFE - GWR

Core concept: GWR is a locally weighted regression method that operates by assigning different weights to observations based on their distance from each query location on the field (Páez et al. 2002).

Simple mathematical framework:

𝑦(location)=𝛽0(location)+𝛽1(location)×treatment(location)+errory(location)=β0(location)+β1(location)×treatment(location)+error
where coefficients 𝛽β vary across the field

“Instead of one average effect across the entire field, GWR estimates unique treatment effects for every location”

Statistical analysis for OFE - GWR

Graphically, GWR involves fitting a spatial kernel to the data as described in the above. (Rakshit et al. (2020))

Statistical analysis for OFE - LMM

Suppose the entire field is partitioned into 𝑝p pseudo-environments (PE), and the 𝑗j-th PE consists of 𝑛𝑗nj grid points, such that the total number of grid points in the experiment is 𝑛=∑𝑝𝑗=1𝑛𝑗n=∑j=1pnj. Let 𝑦𝑗yj be the 𝑛𝑗×1nj×1 vector of observed responses corresponding to the 𝑗j-th PE. The LMM for the combined vector of data 𝑌=[𝑦⊤1,…,𝑦⊤𝑝]⊤Y=[y1⊤,…,yp⊤]⊤ across all PEs is given by

𝑌=𝑋𝜏+𝑍𝑢+𝑒,Y=Xτ+Zu+e,
where 𝜏τ and 𝑢u are 𝑡×1t×1 and 𝑏×1b×1 vectors of fixed and random effects, respectively. The matrices 𝑋X and 𝑍Z are 𝑛×𝑡n×t and 𝑛×𝑏n×b design matrices corresponding to the fixed and random effects, respectively. The 𝑛×1n×1 vector 𝑒e provides the combined residual effects from all PEs.

Statistical analysis for OFE - LMM

Core concept: Linear Mixed Models treat large strip OFEs similar to Multi-Environment Trials (MET), where PE represent different zones within the field.

Simple mathematical framework:

Yield=Zone Effects+Zone × Treatment+Spatial Correlation+ErrorYield=Zone Effects+Zone × Treatment+Spatial Correlation+Error

Why pseudo-environments matter:

  • Fields are not uniform - different zones behave differently

  • One-size-fits-all recommendations often fail

  • Zone-specific management improves overall performance

Practical implementation:

  • Clustering approach using elevation, soil, or other covariates

  • Systematic partitioning when covariates unavailable

  • 3-6 zones typically optimal for most fields

  • Statistical modeling within each zone

Statistical analysis for OFE - LMM

# Example: Progressive model development
# Model 1: Basic

# Model 4: Full spatial (recommended)
lmm_spatial <- asreml(yield ~ zone + at(zone):treatment,

                     data = field_data)

Statistical analysis for OFE - LMM

Interpretation

  • The LMM identifies which treatment yields the highest predicted response in each PE.
  • This enables zone-specific recommendations, supporting precision management.
  • Spatial patterns reflect underlying field heterogeneity and treatment × environment interactions.

Simulation study 2 - LMM

We simulate baseline yield using unconditional Gaussian geo-statistical simulation based on a first-order stationary random field model (Evans et al. (2020)).

𝑧(𝑠)=𝜇+𝜀(𝑠)z(s)=μ+ε(s)
where 𝑧(𝑠)z(s) is the simulated yield at location 𝑠=(𝑥,𝑦)s=(x,y), fertiliser trials are simulated using different designs and is added to the baseline yield:
𝑦(𝑠)=𝑧(𝑠)+𝛽×𝑁(𝑠)y(s)=z(s)+β×N(s)

Simulation workflow:

  1. Simulate baseline yield surfaces using spatial random fields.
  2. Overlay treatment effects using different layouts (strip, stacked).
  3. Fit a range of LMMs with/without spatial terms, using both averaged and full data.
  4. Assess estimation accuracy (relative absolute difference, RAD) and statistical power for each scenario.
  5. Compare performance across all combinations of design, model, and data type.

Simulation study 2 - LMM

Primary research question

How do trial length, number of replications, model structure (spatial vs non-spatial), data granularity (averaged vs full), and layout (strip vs stacked) affect the accuracy and statistical power of treatment effect estimation in OFE?

  • Trial length: Short (10m) to long (110m) strips/trials
  • Replications: 2 to 12
  • Model types: Non-spatial (M11, M21), spatial (M12, M22, M23)
  • Data granularity: Averaged data (mean yield per strip/zone) vs full data (all yield points)
  • Design layouts: Strip and stacked replicated trials

The statistical powers of strip trials

Key findings are

Critical Design Thresholds

Minimum 3 replications required for 95%+ power in most scenarios

Strip length matters: Longer strips (≥≥ 50m) show consistent power improvement

Spatial models (M12, M22) enhance power by 5-15% over non-spatial models

Full data analysis consistently outperforms averaged data approaches

Design-specific performance

Randomised designs: Superior power for hypothesis testing (0.85-1.0 typical)

Systematic designs: Adequate power (0.70-0.95) with operational advantages

Length effect: 110m strips achieve 98%+ power with 2 reps vs 70% for 10m strips

Model selection: Spatial correlation models critical for realistic field conditions

Result 2 - LMM

The statistical powers of stacked trials

Result 2 - LMM

The statistical powers of stacked trials

Key findings are

Critical performance patterns:

  • M21 (Full non-spatial): Consistently achieves 100% power across all scenarios

  • M23 models: Generally poor performance (0.1-0.7 power) - avoid in practice

  • Length sensitivity: Dramatic improvement from 10m (0.0-0.7) to 110m (0.25-1.0) trials

  • Replication effects: Optimal performance at 10-12 reps, diminishing returns beyond 6 reps

Model selection insights:

  • Averaged data models: Highly variable power (0.0-1.0) - unreliable for inference

  • Full data analysis: More consistent and higher power overall

  • Spatial vs non-spatial: Non-spatial models (M21) surprisingly outperform spatial models (M22, M23)

  • Design comparison: Randomised slightly better than systematic, but differences minimal

Practical recommendations: For stacked trials, prioritize M21 models with full data analysis and ensure adequate trial length (≥≥ 50m) for reliable statistical inference.
